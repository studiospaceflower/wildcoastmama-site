5 Healthy One-Hob Meals I Make Every Week in the Van

One hob. Real ingredients. Twenty minutes. Tilly gets a chickpea as a tax on each one. This is non-negotiable.

16 Jun 2026
7 min read

Your photo goes here

I want to be honest with you about van life cooking before we get into the recipes.

It is not all artisan sourdough and aesthetic cast iron skillets arranged beautifully in golden hour light. Some evenings it is standing in a small space with one hob ring, a tin opener, and a deep personal commitment to not having to wash more than two things afterwards.

But here is what I have discovered over months of cooking in Bertha: the constraint is actually useful. When you can only cook on one ring, you stop overcomplicating things. You stop reaching for the elaborate recipe with seventeen steps. You cook simply, with good ingredients, and the food is — genuinely, consistently — better than you expect.

These are the five meals I make every single week without fail. They are quick, they use ingredients that travel well, they are nutritious enough for a pregnant woman living in a van, and they are the kind of food that makes you feel properly fed rather than merely surviving.

Tilly gets a tax on each one. This is not negotiable and has been agreed by all parties.

1. Coastal Lemon & Chickpea One-Pot

This is my most-made meal. I could eat it three times a week and not get bored.

You need:

  • 1 tin chickpeas, drained
  • 1 tin chopped tomatoes
  • 1 whole lemon (zest and juice)
  • 2 cloves garlic, crushed
  • 1 tsp cumin
  • 1 tsp smoked paprika
  • Big handful of kale or spinach
  • Olive oil, salt, pepper

How to make it:

Heat a good glug of olive oil in your pan. Add the garlic and let it soften for a minute — don't let it burn. Add the cumin and smoked paprika and stir for thirty seconds until the spices are fragrant.

Tip in the chickpeas and tomatoes. Add the lemon zest, a good squeeze of juice, salt and pepper. Let it bubble away for about fifteen minutes until it thickens slightly. Stir in the kale or spinach right at the end and let it wilt.

Eat as it is, with bread if you have it, or over rice if you've got a second ring free.

Tilly's tax: One chickpea. She has strong opinions about which chickpea. The process takes longer than you'd expect.

Why I make it every week: It's genuinely one of the most comforting things I know how to cook. It's also high in iron, which matters when you're pregnant and your midwife keeps giving you looks.

2. Ginger & Coconut Lentil Soup

This one saved me in the first trimester when I couldn't face much but needed something warm and nourishing. It has become a permanent fixture.

You need:

  • 150g red lentils
  • 1 tin coconut milk
  • 500ml vegetable stock (a cube is fine)
  • 1 onion, diced
  • 2 cloves garlic
  • Thumb of fresh ginger, grated (or 1 tsp ground ginger)
  • 1 tsp turmeric
  • 1 tsp cumin
  • Juice of half a lime

How to make it:

Soften the onion in oil for five minutes. Add the garlic, ginger, turmeric and cumin and stir for a minute. Add the lentils, coconut milk and stock. Bring to a boil then simmer for twenty minutes, stirring occasionally, until the lentils are completely soft and the soup has thickened.

Squeeze in the lime at the end. Adjust the salt. Done.

Tilly's tax: A small piece of plain carrot from the prep stage. She receives this with the expression of someone who expected better but will accept what is offered.

Why I make it every week: It freezes well if you have a freezer. Make a big batch, freeze portions, and you have emergency meals for the days when cooking feels impossible.

3. Garlic Butter Salmon with Greens

The one that feels like a treat even though it takes fifteen minutes.

You need:

  • 2 salmon fillets
  • Big handful of green beans or tenderstem broccoli
  • 3 cloves garlic, sliced
  • Good knob of butter
  • Juice of half a lemon
  • Salt, pepper

How to make it:

Season the salmon well. Melt the butter in your pan over a medium-high heat. Place the salmon skin-side down and don't touch it for four minutes. Flip it, add the garlic and let it cook for another three minutes.

Remove the salmon and keep it warm. Add the green beans to the buttery garlicky pan with a splash of water. Put the lid on for three minutes. They'll steam perfectly.

Squeeze over the lemon. Put everything on the plate. Done.

Tilly's tax: Nothing. Tilly does not get salmon. She knows this. She still asks. Every single time.

Why I make it every week: Omega-3s. I'm growing a brain. It matters. Also it genuinely tastes like something you'd pay for in a restaurant.

4. Spiced Sweet Potato & Black Bean Bowl

This one is filling in a way that lasts. I make it when I know I'm going to be on my feet for a long time.

You need:

  • 2 medium sweet potatoes, cubed small
  • 1 tin black beans, drained
  • 1 tin chopped tomatoes
  • 1 tsp smoked paprika
  • 1 tsp cumin
  • Half tsp chilli flakes (optional)
  • 1 lime
  • Fresh coriander if you have it

How to make it:

The key to this one is cutting the sweet potato small — 1cm cubes — so it cooks through in about twelve minutes in the pan with a lid on and a splash of water. Once the potato is soft, add the black beans, tomatoes and spices. Simmer for five minutes. Squeeze over the lime, scatter over coriander.

If you want to make it more substantial, a fried egg on top takes it to another level.

Tilly's tax: One small piece of plain sweet potato. She regards this as a reasonable contribution to proceedings.

Why I make it every week: It's one of those meals that is genuinely better the next day cold, which means leftovers are actually something to look forward to.

5. Anchovy & Tomato Pasta

I know anchovies are divisive. I don't care. This is the best fifteen-minute pasta I've ever made and I will defend it.

You need:

  • 200g pasta (something sturdy — rigatoni, penne)
  • 1 tin anchovies in oil
  • 1 tin cherry tomatoes (or chopped)
  • 3 cloves garlic
  • Chilli flakes
  • Handful of parsley if you have it
  • Parmesan to finish

How to make it:

Cook the pasta in well-salted water. While it cooks, heat the anchovy oil in a separate pan. Add the garlic and chilli and let them sizzle. Add the anchovies and stir — they'll dissolve into the oil entirely, leaving behind this deeply savoury base. Add the tomatoes and simmer.

Drain the pasta, keeping a cup of the starchy water. Add the pasta to the sauce with a splash of the water and toss everything together.

Parmesan on top. Parsley if you have it.

Tilly's tax: Nothing from this one. She sniffed the anchovy tin and withdrew with dignity.

Why I make it every week: It uses tins from the back of the cupboard. It comes together in the time it takes the pasta to cook. It tastes like something that took far longer than it did.

A note on van life cooking generally

The thing nobody tells you about cooking in a small space is that you get very good at it very quickly. Not in a showy way. In a practical way. You learn to prep as you go so there's no pile of boards and bowls waiting. You learn which ingredients earn their space and which ones don't. You learn that a really good knife and one decent heavy pan are worth more than a full rack of equipment.

You also learn, fairly quickly, that a golden retriever positioned optimally near the hob is an excellent sous chef, quality control manager, and general morale booster.

Even if she does keep asking about the salmon.

— Wild Coast Mama
