6 High-Protein One-Hob Meals on a Budget

Cheap, filling, and genuinely high in protein — no fancy equipment, no expensive ingredients, just real food that keeps you going.

30 Jun 2026
7 min read

Your photo goes here

Protein is the thing I think about most when I'm cooking these days. Growing a person apparently requires quite a lot of it, and the easy, lazy version of van life eating — bread, pasta, whatever's quickest — does not deliver on that front nearly as well as it needs to.

So these are the six meals I've built up that genuinely tick three boxes at once: properly high in protein, cheap enough that cooking every day doesn't quietly destroy the budget, and simple enough to manage on one hob ring without a single piece of specialist equipment.

Tilly remains involved in quality control throughout. Her standards have not lowered.

1. Tinned Tuna & White Bean Mash

This sounds unglamorous and tastes considerably better than it sounds. It has become one of my genuine favourites.

You need:

  • 2 tins tuna in oil, drained
  • 1 tin cannellini or butter beans, drained
  • 2 cloves garlic
  • Juice of 1 lemon
  • Olive oil
  • Handful of parsley or any green herb

How to make it:

Warm the beans gently with the garlic and a splash of olive oil for a few minutes. Roughly mash them with a fork — you want texture, not a smooth puree. Stir through the tuna, lemon juice, and herbs. Season well.

Eat warm, with bread if you have it, or just as it is from the pan.

Protein per serving: roughly 35-40g. Cost: under £2.50 a serving.

Tilly's tax: A small amount of plain tuna. She considers this the single best recipe on the list and has communicated this clearly.

2. Egg Fried Rice with Frozen Peas

Eggs are one of the best value protein sources going, and this uses a full four of them without feeling like an omelette.

You need:

  • 4 eggs
  • 250g pre-cooked rice (the microwave pouches work well, or leftover rice)
  • Handful frozen peas
  • 2 cloves garlic
  • Soy sauce
  • Splash of sesame oil if you have it

How to make it:

Beat the eggs and scramble them quickly in a hot pan, breaking them into small pieces. Remove and set aside. In the same pan, fry the garlic briefly, add the rice and peas, and stir-fry for a few minutes until heated through. Stir the eggs back in, add a good splash of soy sauce, and toss everything together.

Protein per serving: roughly 22-25g. Cost: under £1.50 a serving.

Tilly's tax: One pea. She makes a point of how little this is.

3. Chicken Thigh & Chickpea Curry

Chicken thighs are considerably cheaper than breast and, cooked properly, taste better too. This is the meal I make when I want something that feels like a proper dinner.

You need:

  • 4 boneless chicken thighs, cut into chunks
  • 1 tin chickpeas, drained
  • 1 tin chopped tomatoes
  • 1 onion, diced
  • 2 tsp curry powder
  • 1 tsp turmeric
  • Garlic, ginger if you have it

How to make it:

Brown the chicken in a little oil for five minutes. Add the onion and soften. Add the spices and stir for a minute, then add the chickpeas and tomatoes. Simmer for twenty minutes with the lid on until the chicken is cooked through and the sauce has thickened.

Protein per serving: roughly 40g. Cost: around £2 a serving.

Tilly's tax: One chickpea, plain. The chicken is off limits and she knows it, though this does not stop her asking with her whole body.

4. Lentil & Halloumi One-Pan

Halloumi is not cheap exactly, but a little goes a long way, and combined with lentils this is a genuinely substantial, protein-rich meal.

You need:

  • 1 tin lentils, drained (or 100g dried, pre-cooked)
  • Half a block halloumi, sliced
  • 1 tin chopped tomatoes
  • 1 tsp cumin
  • 1 tsp smoked paprika
  • Spinach or kale

How to make it:

Fry the halloumi slices in a dry pan until golden on both sides, then remove and set aside. In the same pan, add the lentils, tomatoes and spices, and simmer for ten minutes. Stir in the greens at the end and let them wilt. Top with the halloumi.

Protein per serving: roughly 28-30g. Cost: around £2.50 a serving.

Tilly's tax: She is fascinated by halloumi and deeply offended that she does not get any.

5. Tinned Mackerel & New Potato Salad

This one needs almost no cooking, which is exactly what you want on the hottest or most exhausted days.

You need:

  • 2 tins mackerel fillets (plain or peppered)
  • 400g new potatoes, halved
  • A handful of green beans
  • 1 tbsp Greek yoghurt or mayo
  • 1 tsp mustard
  • Lemon juice

How to make it:

Boil the potatoes for 12-15 minutes until tender, adding the green beans for the last 4 minutes. Drain. Mix the yoghurt, mustard and lemon juice into a quick dressing. Toss the warm potatoes and beans through it, then top with the mackerel, roughly flaked.

Protein per serving: roughly 30g. Cost: under £2 a serving.

Tilly's tax: Nothing. She is suspicious of mackerel on principle but watches very closely regardless.

6. Cottage Cheese & Bean Jacket-Style Pan

This is the meal I make when I genuinely cannot be bothered, and it still delivers properly.

You need:

  • 2 large potatoes, cubed small (skip the jacket, save the time)
  • 1 tin baked beans
  • 200g cottage cheese
  • Spring onion or chives if you have them
  • Black pepper

How to make it:

Cube the potatoes small and cook them in the pan with a lid on and a splash of water for about 12 minutes until tender. Warm the beans separately or in the same pan once the potatoes are done. Serve with a generous dollop of cottage cheese on top and a good amount of black pepper.

Protein per serving: roughly 25g, mostly from the cottage cheese. Cost: under £1.50 a serving.

Tilly's tax: One small piece of plain potato. She regards cottage cheese with deep suspicion and has never asked for any.

A note on why protein matters more than people think

I'm not a nutritionist, and I'm not going to pretend to give you medical advice. But what I've learned, both from being pregnant and from generally trying to feel less wrecked at the end of a day, is that protein is the thing that actually keeps you full and steady, in a way that bread and pasta alone simply don't.

None of these recipes are expensive, complicated, or require anything beyond a single hob ring and a reasonably sharp knife. That, more than anything, is the point.

— Wild Coast Mama

Tilly's note I have reviewed all six recipes for the second time this month. My tax has been collected appropriately on four occasions. The mackerel and cottage cheese situations remain unresolved and I consider this an ongoing matter.
